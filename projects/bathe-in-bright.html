Bathe In Bright

14–24 November 2024 — Macleay Valley Community Art Gallery, Gladstone NSW

This exhibition of collaged inks shares a dreamed utopia, and the disruption of that vision by a recurring worming memory — the violation of a true earthly paradise.

My intention was to save and share an ethereal, other-worldly tranquility that I experienced in the South Pacific Islands of Tahiti — a place frequently referred to as a paradise on Earth.

I read accounts written at the time of European discovery and colonisation, papers written about the culture and history, and novels that evoke a similar feeling of a dazzling paradise. I recalled my research into the island’s mythology and culture while studying Tahiti in a 1980’s art school theory subject; early childhood delight listening to stories of the Pacific Islands; the promise of an exotic and exclusive paradise suggested by the Cussins Imperial Leather Soap TV ad; the potential to be self sufficient in abundant welcoming isolation like Gilligan’s Island; and, of course, the veneration of island life presented by Gaugin, Matisse, and many other artists.

I wanted to express this feeling of a utopia as an essence of my own experience — the clean light, a gentle telepathic silence, the drifting fragrance of flowers, in a vast and isolated blueness of sky, lagoon and ocean. The aura of a proud, golden population living at the breast of the Earth, the moon, their Gods, their garden home.

To avoid parroting the familiar imagery of the European aesthetic, the Kitsch, the “Dusky Maiden”, the directly re-presented, I cut up a descriptive passage from R L Stevenson’s In The South Seas, and randomly rearranged the words and phrases.

Following the newly written passage as loose instructions for processes and imagery prompts I…

bath(ed) the paper in bright yellow ink
added washes of blue and green one of these the ground
made prints of my palm for beneath the palms
drew flowers for bloom appeared

I used ink washes as spacious environments and populated them with collaged golden paper dolls living in this ethereal other world, backs turned, resting, relaxing, sleeping and floating: vulnerable, though aware of our presence.

I interpreted the blue of the clove as a homonym — the Blew of the Clove. For several months I had avoided the persistent memory that the Polynesian Islands were forced to host the French Atomic Bomb tests. Then, like the eroding concrete caps on radioactive waste dumps, my resolve disintegrated and reality leaked through. The pacific vision interrupted, the collaged cutting out became as much a cutting into. Colours moved from romantic washes of moonlight and dawn to harsher exploded daylight fracturing (jewels pencilled out the lighter on the more dark).

I remember ‘Oro — Polynesian god of wars, volcanoes, lightening and fine art: destruction and creation. The audacious experimentation by the French authorities of 193 nuclear test explosions between July 1966 and January 1996 was, however, only to gain military superiority. Local inhabitants were not informed. The environmental and health effects were disguised and denied. This was the knowing destruction and poisoning of an Eden. Resultant radioactivity seeps through the ocean and into all of our lives today.

Repurposing failed backgrounds and recycling paper off cuts became a substantial creative consideration during the making of these artworks and suggests the obvious way forward — to work with that which remains.
